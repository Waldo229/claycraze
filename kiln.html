Kilns

The fire that completes the work.

The ClaycrazE studio operates two kilns. Each serves a different role in the exploration of clay and fire, balancing variation and control.

The first kiln is "Poor Boy", a Skutt 1027 electric kiln converted to gas firing. This kiln has been the heart of the studio for years. Like many fuel-fired kilns, it produces uneven heat between top and bottom, creating variations that can be both challenging and rewarding. Learning the behavior of this kiln has been central to the studio practice.

The second kiln is a modern Skutt 1027 programmable electric kiln. It replaced an older Gare electric kiln and introduces precision and repeatability to the studio. The kiln includes a full digital control system and is mounted on a rolling stand with locking wheels for flexibility within the workspace.

The kiln was purchased from Clayworks in Richmond, Virginia, who also delivered and installed the unit. Their support and expertise made the transition seamless.

One goal of the GENE project is to monitor the firing conditions of “Poor Boy” while the programmable Skutt kiln runs alongside it. By tracking temperatures in both kilns, it becomes possible to compare behavior and better understand the thermal layering that develops in the gas kiln.

Before these kilns were part of the studio, much of the firing was done outdoors using a simple weed burner to reach temperatures between cone 6 and cone 10+. Over time, the process was refined with the addition of a pyrometer, allowing observation of rate-of-rise while still relying on pyrometric cones to tell the real story.

The mechanical Kiln Sitter remains part of the ritual. When the arm drops, signaling that the cone has bent and the kiln has reached temperature, it is a sound every potter recognizes — a quiet confirmation that the firing has completed its work.
