My Kilns

The fire that completes the work.

The ClaycrazE studio currently operates two kilns. Each serves a different role in the exploration of clay and fire.

The first kiln is "Poor Boy", a Skutt 1027 electric kiln that I converted to gas firing. This kiln has been the heart of the studio for years. Like many fuel-fired kilns, it produces uneven heat between top and bottom, creating variations that can be both challenging and rewarding. Learning the behavior of this kiln has been an important part of the studio practice.

The second kiln is a modern Skutt 1027 programmable electric kiln. This kiln replaced an older Gare electric kiln that had served the studio for many years. The new kiln includes the full range of Skutt controls and conveniences, including a rolling stand with locking wheels.

The kiln was purchased from Clayworks in Richmond, Virginia. Clayworks delivered and installed the kiln and I am greateful for their support and expert advice.

One goal of the GENE project is to monitor the firing conditions of “Poor Boy” while the programmable Skutt kiln runs alongside it. By tracking temperatures in both kilns, it should become possible to synchronize firings and better understand the thermal layering that develops in the older gas kiln.

Before these kilns were part of the studio, much of the firing was done outdoors using a simple weed burner to reach temperatures between cone 6 and cone 10+. Over time I refined the process with the addition of a pyrometer, allowing me to observe the rate of temperature rise while still relying on the honesty of pyrometric cones to tell the real story.

The mechanical Kiln Sitter is still part of the ritual. When the arm drops, signaling that the cone has bent and the kiln has reached temperature, it is a sound every potter recognizes — quiet confirmation that the firing has completed its work.

The Kiln Sitter will soon be augmented with a wireless thermocouple connected to an ESP32 sensor, extending that old mechanical signal into the digital world.
