Places

Clay, land, and origin.

Clay begins in the ground. Before it becomes a vessel, it exists as part of a larger landscape shaped by water, pressure, and time.

In Tidewater Virginia, clay is tied to river systems — the James, the York, and their tributaries — where deposits form and settle over long cycles. These materials carry the history of movement, erosion, and accumulation.

Long before European settlement, Indigenous cultures including the Chickahominy and Pamunkey peoples worked these materials into functional and ceremonial forms. Their practices continue today, maintaining a direct relationship between land and making.

Recovered Fragments

Colonial and imported ceramics

Tidewater Virginia

Material Context

Fragments recovered from Jamestown reflect a convergence of traditions — European, Indigenous, and later global influences. These remains provide a record of adaptation, trade, and survival.

The presence of imported wares alongside local materials reveals both dependence and innovation, situating early American ceramics within a broader global exchange.

The studio draws from this layered context. Local materials, historical reference, and broader ceramic traditions are not treated as separate influences, but as parts of a continuous field.

Chinese, Persian, and African traditions each contribute to an understanding of form and surface — not as decoration, but as expressions of material behavior shaped by firing conditions and use.

Place is not background. It is the starting condition.
