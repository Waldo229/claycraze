Land and clay.

Clay begins in the ground. The Tidewater region of Virginia rests on layers of coastal plain sediment laid down over long geological time. Along the York River near Yorktown, these deposits contain clay-bearing soils that historically supported brickmaking and utilitarian pottery. Working with clay from this landscape connects the finished vessel directly to the place from which it came.

Long before colonial pottery workshops appeared in Yorktown, the Chickahominy and Pamunkey peoples were shaping vessels from the clays of these riverbanks. Pamunkey pottery traditions in particular continue today, preserving methods that reach far back into the region’s past. Their work reminds us that clay in Tidewater Virginia carries a cultural history that precedes European settlement by many centuries.

By the eighteenth century the town of York had become home to working potters such as William Rogers, sometimes described in period records as the “poor potter of Yorktown.” The phrase likely reflected the modest status of a tradesman rather than his ability. Rogers represents the early colonial craft tradition of the region — producing practical wares from local clay for everyday life in the Tidewater economy.

The ClaycrazE studio exists within this layered landscape of material and tradition. Local clays, regional history, and the broader culture of ceramics all inform the work produced here, linking contemporary studio practice to the long story of clay in the York–James river watershed.
