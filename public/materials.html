Theory

Materials

Materials carry memory. Clay bodies, slips, glazes, ash, iron, feldspar, and local earth all behave according to their own character under heat.

My interest is less in novelty than in understanding: how surfaces soften, break, pool, dry, resist, or reveal the clay beneath them. What appears simple on the finished pot is often the result of long attention to small differences.

Material judgment grows slowly. It comes from repeated handling, repeated firing, and from learning to notice what a clay or glaze is inclined to do.

The study of materials is inseparable from the life of the studio. A clay body is not just a recipe, nor a glaze merely a surface coating. Each is part of a larger conversation involving forming, drying, firing, and use.

I am especially drawn to surfaces that feel quiet, settled, and durable—surfaces that do not shout over the form, but deepen it. In that sense, materials are not ornamental additions to the work; they are part of the work’s character from the beginning.

The aim is not technical display for its own sake, but a closer fit between matter, method, and intention. A useful pot should feel as though its surface belongs to it.
