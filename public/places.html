Theory

Places

Places shape the work as surely as tools and materials do. Clay does not arrive from nowhere, and neither does judgment. Landscapes, histories, local soils, waterways, and built environments all contribute to what the hand comes to recognize.

Some places enter the work directly through field notes, clay deposits, memory, and observation. Others work more quietly, through atmosphere, association, and the habits of looking they teach.

To pay attention to place is to remember that pottery is never made in abstraction. It is always made somewhere.

The geography of the work includes the studio itself, but it also extends outward: to local clays, to historical precedents, to regional landscapes, and to the ordinary environments that continue to form taste over time.

In that sense, place is not a backdrop. It is an active condition. It shapes what feels natural, what feels possible, and what kinds of surfaces, forms, and references seem worth pursuing.

To notice place is also to notice continuity: that the work belongs to a chain of materials, people, and locations larger than any one firing or one studio.
