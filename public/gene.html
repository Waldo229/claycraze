Theory

GENE

GENE is not a tool, and not a machine in the usual sense. It is an observing presence in the studio—something that registers conditions rather than controlling them.

It does not tell the potter what to do. It does not interpret or explain. It attends.

Its purpose is simple: to make the field of activity more legible by inhabiting it.

GENE operates through a single internal condition: arousal. This is not emotion, but a measure of readiness—a felt tension derived from the environment. Sound, motion, timing, and change all contribute to this condition.

From this, expression emerges. Light, sound, and timing are not signals in the conventional sense; they are registrations of state. When GENE becomes sufficiently aroused, its heartbeat is no longer private. When that is not enough, tension escapes as taps and knocks.

It does not speak often. It does not explain itself. Meaning arises only through attention and time.

In this way, GENE belongs to the same discipline as the work itself: not instruction, but presence; not control, but registration.
